How Potential Intensity is Read from a Sounding

Potential intensity (PI) is a hurricane's speed limit — the maximum near-surface wind speed a tropical cyclone could reach in a given ocean and atmosphere (Emanuel 1986; Rousseau-Rizzi & Emanuel 2019). The idea is elegant: a hurricane runs as a heat engine on the warmth of the sea, and the energy it can draw from the air–sea temperature and moisture contrast sets a ceiling on how strong its winds can get. This tool computes PI from an editable sounding and draws the construction behind it — adjust any control and the sounding, and every quantity on it, updates instantly.

Reading the construction on the sounding

The chart renders the three profiles the PI closure compares. The magenta curve is the density temperature Tρ of a parcel saturated at the sea-surface temperature, lifted reversibly; the grey curve is Tρ of the actual, subsaturated boundary-layer parcel. Buoyancy is evaluated against the environmental density (virtual) temperature, the dotted red profile. The purple area is the saturation CAPE of the ocean parcel (CAPE*); the red area is the boundary-layer CAPE (CAPEb) that is subtracted; the residual purple — their difference — is what scales V². The saturated parcel's level of neutral buoyancy fixes the outflow temperature T0 (marked), and the SST sits at the surface.

Crucially, PI is not CAPE. A moist-neutral environment can have CAPEb ≈ 0 (the boundary-layer parcel is non-buoyant) yet still support a large PI, because the closure depends on the difference CAPE* − CAPEb — i.e. on the air–sea thermodynamic disequilibrium between a saturated ocean surface and the subsaturated overlying air, not on the environmental instability itself. The "Idealized 30 °C moist adiabat" preset isolates exactly this limit.

The governing relation

An axisymmetric, steady-state tropical cyclone is idealized as a Carnot cycle: isothermal enthalpy uptake from the ocean in the inflow, near-adiabatic ascent in the eyewall, isothermal heat export near the cold outflow, and adiabatic subsidence in the environment. Equating the cycle's mechanical power generation to surface frictional dissipation yields the closure evaluated here (Bister & Emanuel 2002):

Vmax² = (Ck/CD) · (Ts/T0) · (CAPE* − CAPEb)| RMW

where the CAPE difference is evaluated for parcels lifted at the radius of maximum winds. The factor Ts/T0 is the dissipative-heating amplification (it reduces to unity when dissipative heating is disabled). This is mathematically equivalent to the familiar air–sea interaction form

Vmax² = (Ck/CD) · (Ts − T0)/T0 · (k0* − k)

with (Ts−T0)/T0 the Carnot efficiency (including dissipative heating) and k0* − k the air–sea enthalpy disequilibrium — the deficit between the saturation enthalpy of the sea surface and the enthalpy of near-surface air. The CAPE difference at the radius of maximum winds is the numerical embodiment of efficiency × disequilibrium; the pcmin algorithm evaluates it directly from the sounding rather than parameterizing k0* − k.

Ck/CD
Exchange-coefficient ratio

Ratio of the surface exchange coefficients for enthalpy and momentum. Field estimates place it near 0.9; it remains uncertain, particularly at hurricane-force wind speeds.

Ts/T0
Thermodynamic efficiency

Dissipative heating recycles boundary-layer dissipation as a surface heat source, replacing the nominal Carnot factor (Ts−T0)/Ts with (Ts−T0)/T0 — roughly a 20% intensification of Vmax.

CAPE*−CAPEb
Disequilibrium term

Saturation CAPE of the SST parcel minus boundary-layer CAPE, both at the RMW. Proportional to k0*−k; the residual-purple area on the sounding.

T0
Outflow temperature

Temperature at the level of neutral buoyancy of the SST-saturated parcel — the cold reservoir of the cycle. Diagnosed, not prescribed.

The pcmin closure, step by step

For a given SST and sounding the algorithm iterates on the minimum central pressure pc. Each iteration (i) lifts a parcel saturated at the SST from min(pc, 1000 hPa) — i.e. at the radius of maximum winds, where the depressed pressure raises the saturation mixing ratio — to obtain CAPE* and, from its level of neutral buoyancy, T0 and the outflow level; (ii) lifts the boundary-layer parcel, with mixing ratio adjusted to the reduced pressure, for CAPEb; and (iii) updates pc = penv·exp[−CAT/(Rd Tv,avg)], where CAT collects the scaled CAPE difference. Convergence (Δpc < 0.5 hPa) takes a few iterations. Buoyancy uses density temperature Tρ = T(1 + rv/ε)/(1 + rT); the 10-m wind is reduced from the gradient value by a factor 0.8 (Emanuel 2000; Powell et al. 2003). The implementation is a direct JavaScript port of tcpyPI (Gilford 2021), reproducing the Python reference to within 0.01 m/s and 0.01 hPa.

Reversible vs pseudo-adiabatic ascent

Under reversible ascent (the default) all condensate is retained by the parcel, so the total water mixing ratio rT exceeds the vapor rv and condensate (water) loading depresses Tρ aloft — reducing parcel buoyancy, CAPE, and PI relative to the pseudo-adiabatic limit, in which condensate is removed (rT = rv). This is why density temperature, not virtual temperature, is the proper buoyancy variable for the parcels: it generalizes to the condensate-bearing case. The two assumptions bracket the achievable intensity; the true value depends on hydrometeor fallout, which neither idealization resolves.

Environmental controls

SST. Raising Ts increases both the efficiency and the saturation enthalpy k0*, so PI rises steeply and super-linearly — the basis for projected increases in the intensity ceiling under warming, modulated by changes in outflow temperature. Outflow / upper troposphere. A colder T0 widens the efficiency factor; PI is sensitive to tropopause-region temperature. Boundary-layer humidity. A drier inflow increases k0* − k (larger disequilibrium) but the net PI response is moderated through the central-pressure feedback. Mid-tropospheric humidity influences CAPEb and the sub-saturation structure but not the air–sea disequilibrium directly. PI is an energetic upper bound; observed storms typically realize 50–80% of it owing to unbalanced dynamics, shear, ocean coupling, and transient structure.
