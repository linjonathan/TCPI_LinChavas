How the speed limit works

A mature hurricane is a natural Carnot heat engine. It draws heat from the warm ocean at the sea surface, carries that energy upward and outward, and exhausts it to space near the cold tropopause. The bigger the temperature gap between the warm intake and the cold exhaust, the more of that heat the storm can convert into the mechanical energy of wind. Potential intensity (PI) is the wind speed at which the energy the storm generates exactly balances the energy it loses to friction at the sea surface.

The four strokes

Air spirals inward along the sea surface, picking up heat and moisture from the ocean (isothermal intake). It then turns upward in the eyewall, ascending nearly without heat exchange (moist adiabat). High up it flows outward and radiates heat to space at the cold outflow temperature, before sinking far away to complete the loop. The efficiency of this cycle is set by the intake and exhaust temperatures.

The governing equation

Setting the engine's power output equal to frictional dissipation gives the potential intensity:

Vmax²  =  (Ck/CD) · (Ts − T0) / T0 · (k0* − k)

In words: the squared wind speed equals an air–sea exchange ratio, times the Carnot efficiency, times the thermodynamic disequilibrium between the ocean surface and the air just above it. Each piece is a knob you can turn in the tool above.

Ck/CD
Exchange ratio

How efficiently the sea surface gives up heat versus how strongly it drags on the wind. Observations put it near 0.9.

(Ts−T0)/T0
Carnot efficiency

Warm sea-surface intake Ts minus cold outflow T0, divided by the outflow temperature. A colder tropopause means a more efficient engine.

k0*−k
Air–sea disequilibrium

The gap between the saturation enthalpy of the ocean surface and the actual enthalpy of near-surface air — the fuel the storm extracts.

T0
Outflow temperature

Found by lifting a saturated surface parcel to its level of neutral buoyancy. The tool reports it above.

What the calculation actually does

This tool ports Kerry Emanuel's pcmin algorithm (as implemented in the tcpyPI Python package) directly to JavaScript. For a given sea-surface temperature and sounding it: (1) lifts a parcel saturated at the sea-surface temperature and integrates its buoyancy to get the saturation CAPE and the outflow temperature; (2) lifts a boundary-layer parcel to get the environmental CAPE; and (3) iterates on the central pressure until the difference between those energies, scaled by the Carnot efficiency and the exchange ratio, converges. The 10-metre wind is then reduced from the gradient-wind value by a factor of 0.8. The JavaScript port reproduces the Python reference to within 0.01 m/s and 0.01 hPa across test soundings.

Why your knobs move the answer

SST ↑ raises both the Carnot efficiency and the air–sea disequilibrium, so PI climbs steeply — roughly exponentially — which is why warming oceans raise the ceiling on hurricane strength. A colder upper atmosphere (lower outflow temperature) widens the Carnot gap and also raises PI. A drier boundary layer increases the disequilibrium k0*−k and raises PI. Dissipative heating recycles frictional heat back into the engine, multiplying efficiency by Ts/T0 and adding roughly 20% to peak winds. Note that real storms rarely reach their potential intensity — PI is a ceiling, not a forecast.
